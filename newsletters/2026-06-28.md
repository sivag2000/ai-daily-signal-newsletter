# AI at the Crossroads
**Issue #033 · June 28, 2026**
*Navigating the regulatory tightrope as advanced AI models face government scrutiny and global markets shift.*

## TL;DR
-   **Government requests** prompt OpenAI to limit GPT-5.6 rollout, echoing Anthropic's earlier Mythos restrictions.
-   **OpenAI advances custom chips** with Broadcom, signaling a broader industry push away from Nvidia dominance.
-   **Asian AI startups** launch "Mythos-like" models, capitalizing on U.S. export restrictions and shifting global access.

## THE BIG STORY
The AI world is grappling with escalating government intervention, as regulatory bodies increasingly influence the rollout of advanced models. Following weeks of negotiations and an earlier export ban on Anthropic's **Mythos** model, the Trump Administration has now requested OpenAI to limit the public release of its highly anticipated **GPT-5.6 Sol**. This new model, previewed as a next-generation system with stronger capabilities in coding, science, and cybersecurity, is currently only accessible to select trusted partners.

This coordinated government action underscores a new era where national security and control over powerful AI capabilities are paramount, even at the cost of broader public and developer access. OpenAI explicitly stated, "We don't believe this kind of government access process should become the long-term default. It keeps the best tools from users, developers, enterprises, cyber defenders, and global partners who need them." The restrictions have created a significant gap in the global market, particularly impacting non-U.S. developers and enterprises reliant on leading American AI models.

The implications are far-reaching, transforming the competitive landscape. With Anthropic's **Mythos** also restricted to a select group of over **100 U.S. companies and government agencies**—including their non-American employees—and **GPT-5.6 Sol** similarly constrained, innovation outside the U.S. is seeking alternatives. This environment is already fueling the rise of independent AI development in regions like Asia, which now see an opportunity to establish their own leading models free from U.S. export concerns, potentially creating lasting market shifts.

## MORE SIGNALS
### OpenAI's Hardware Play
OpenAI is aggressively pursuing custom silicon, collaborating with Broadcom to unveil **Jalapeño**, a new AI chip optimized for large language model (LLM) inference. This move is part of a broader industry trend where major AI players, including SpaceX, are developing proprietary chips to reduce dependency on traditional GPU providers like Nvidia and to enhance performance and efficiency across their AI systems. This push signals a strategic shift towards integrated hardware-software ecosystems for competitive advantage.

### Talent Exodus to OpenAI
Apple's Vice President in charge of the **Vision Pro** headset, Paul Meade, is reportedly leaving Apple to join OpenAI's nascent hardware team. This high-profile talent acquisition highlights OpenAI's deepening ambition beyond software models, suggesting a strategic move into hardware innovation or agentic device development. The departure of a key executive from Apple's flagship mixed-reality product underscores the intense competition for top AI talent and OpenAI's growing allure.

### Asian AI Firms Seize Opportunity
Amid U.S. government-imposed restrictions on access to advanced models like Anthropic's **Mythos**, Asian AI startups are rapidly launching their own "Mythos-like" models. These new offerings promise comparable capabilities without the fear of U.S. export bans, directly challenging the dominance of American AI labs in vast global markets. This rapid response could irrevocably alter the global distribution of AI leadership and investment, as Europe and other regions also express interest in hosting AI firms independent of U.S. control.
