# AI's Shifting Tides
**Issue #034 · June 29, 2026**
*Navigating the regulatory currents and emergent capabilities shaping AI's next wave.*

## TL;DR
-   **US government limits major AI model rollouts**, raising concerns over access and market competitiveness.
-   **OpenAI and Broadcom unveil a custom AI chip** for LLM inference, signaling a hardware optimization push.
-   **Ford rehires human engineers** after AI alone proved insufficient for high-quality product development.

## THE BIG STORY
The US government has notably intervened in the release of advanced AI models, impacting both Anthropic and OpenAI. After initial restrictions, the **Trump Administration allowed Anthropic's Mythos 5** to be used by over **100 US companies and government agencies**, including their non-American employees. However, the path was not smooth; Anthropic previously had to take its most advanced models offline for weeks due to White House negotiations, sparking fears among international partners.

Following this, **OpenAI confirmed limitations on its GPT-5.6 Sol rollout** after a similar government request. OpenAI expressed concern, stating, "We don’t believe this kind of government access process should become the long-term default." The company argues that such restrictions prevent the best tools from reaching users, developers, enterprises, and cybersecurity defenders who need them. This intervention highlights a growing tension between rapid AI innovation and governmental oversight—a dynamic that risks hindering global access and fostering resentment.

The immediate consequence? Asian AI startups are now launching "Mythos-like" models without the fear of export bans, potentially allowing them to capture an enormous market that US AI labs might struggle to reclaim. This governmental pause, intended for security or control, could paradoxically accelerate fragmentation in the global AI landscape, shifting the center of gravity for advanced model development.

## MORE SIGNALS
### Ford Rehires 'Gray Beard' Engineers
Ford is reportedly re-hiring experienced "gray beard" engineers after its initial strategy to rely solely on AI for product development fell short. A company executive admitted, "Mistakenly we thought that by just introducing artificial intelligence... that would produce a high-quality product." This move underscores a critical realization: while AI can augment human capabilities, deep human expertise—especially in complex engineering—remains indispensable for quality assurance and innovation, highlighting AI's current limitations in fully autonomous, high-stakes creative roles.

### Wall Street Eyes Micron as the Next Nvidia
Wall Street investors are increasingly looking to **Micron Technology** as a potential "next Nvidia" due to the surging demand for high-bandwidth memory (HBM) essential for AI workloads. As AI models grow larger and more complex, the need for specialized memory that can rapidly feed data to AI accelerators like Nvidia’s GPUs intensifies. This speculation reflects the broader market's scramble to identify public companies poised to capitalize on the foundational hardware demands of the AI boom, beyond just chip designers.

### OpenAI's Strategic Hardware and Talent Moves
OpenAI is intensifying its focus on hardware, a critical bottleneck for scaling AI. The company, in collaboration with **Broadcom**, unveiled **Jalapeño**, a new custom AI chip specifically designed for LLM inference. This move aims to improve performance, efficiency, and scale across AI systems. Further bolstering its hardware ambitions, **Paul Meade**, the Apple vice president overseeing the **Vision Pro** headset, is reportedly leaving to join OpenAI’s hardware team. These actions signal OpenAI’s strategic intent to integrate deeply into the physical infrastructure of AI, moving beyond just software models.

## WHAT TO WATCH
The ongoing tug-of-war between government requests for AI model restrictions and the industry's drive for broader access will define global AI development trajectories. Expect further innovation in agentic AI, as demonstrated by Google's Gemini updates, and a continued push towards specialized hardware like OpenAI's Jalapeño chip to optimize performance. Furthermore, Anthropic's "2028 AI Warning" about recursive self-improvement signals a longer-term, critical discussion on AI safety and control, hinting at future regulatory landscapes.
