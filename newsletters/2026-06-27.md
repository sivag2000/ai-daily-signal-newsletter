# AI Crossroads: Regulation & Race
**Issue #032 · June 27, 2026**
*Government oversight shapes a competitive global AI landscape.*

## TL;DR
- US government restricted **OpenAI's GPT-5.6** rollout and **Anthropic's Mythos** access due to safety concerns.
- This creates an opportunity for Asian AI startups to launch competitive **Mythos-like** models globally.
- Major AI players, including OpenAI, are developing custom chips like **Jalapeño** to reduce reliance on Nvidia.

## THE BIG STORY
The US government has fundamentally reshaped the landscape for frontier AI model deployment, directly intervening to limit access to advanced models from leading developers. This week, the White House requested that OpenAI restrict the public rollout of its newest model, **GPT-5.6 Sol**, citing safety concerns. This follows a similar intervention where Anthropic's powerful **Mythos 5** model was initially withheld from broader release, only to be later authorized for use by a select group of over **100** US companies and government agencies, including their non-American employees.

This unprecedented level of government involvement marks a critical turning point for the AI industry. OpenAI explicitly stated, "We don’t believe this kind of government access process should become the long-term default," arguing it prevents the best tools from reaching "users, developers, enterprises, cyber defenders, and global partners who need them." Such restrictions introduce significant friction into the innovation cycle and raise questions about the balance between rapid technological advancement and national security interests.

The consequences extend beyond US borders, profoundly affecting global market dynamics. With leading US models facing domestic export restrictions, a vacuum is created, which Asian AI startups are swiftly moving to fill. These companies are launching their own **Mythos-like** models, promising advanced capabilities without the threat of a US export ban. This strategic shift could lead to a permanent realignment of the global AI market, potentially costing US AI labs an enormous and irreplaceable market share in a critical developing sector.

## MORE SIGNALS
### Custom Chips Challenge Nvidia
Major AI developers are rapidly moving to design their own specialized AI chips, intensifying competition in the hardware sector. OpenAI, for instance, has partnered with Broadcom to unveil **Jalapeño**, a custom chip optimized for **LLM inference**—the process of running AI models to generate responses. This trend, also seen at companies like SpaceX, aims to improve performance and efficiency while reducing the heavy reliance on external GPU providers, primarily Nvidia, for compute infrastructure.

### Asia's AI Ambition
Amid US regulatory actions affecting models like **Anthropic's Mythos**, Asian AI startups are seizing the opportunity to launch their own advanced models. These new platforms promise capabilities on par with top-tier models, free from US export controls. This strategic move could fundamentally alter the global AI landscape, potentially allowing Asian companies to capture significant market share and establish new regional AI hubs, diminishing the long-term dominance of US-based AI labs.

### Agentic AI Transforms Work
New research from OpenAI and updates to Google's **Gemini** highlight the growing power of **AI agents**—systems capable of performing complex, multi-step tasks autonomously. OpenAI's paper details how these agents are enabling longer, more intricate workflows, significantly expanding productivity across various professional roles. Concurrently, Google announced that the **Gemini app** is becoming more "agentic," offering proactive, **24/7** assistance and new tools tailored for businesses and entrepreneurs, signaling a shift towards more autonomous and integrated AI support.

The accelerating pace of AI innovation will continue to clash with increasing governmental desire for oversight and control. Expect continued global debates on responsible deployment, the rise of regional AI powerhouses, and intensified competition in custom hardware as AI's geopolitical stakes grow. The balance between open access and controlled deployment remains precarious.
